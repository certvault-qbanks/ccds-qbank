How to Pass the CCDS Exam in 2026: Complete Study Guide

Updated April 2026 • 12 min read

The Certified Clinical Documentation Specialist (CCDS) exam is one of the most respected credentials in clinical documentation integrity (CDI). Offered by the Association of Clinical Documentation Integrity Specialists (ACDIS), it validates your ability to apply coding guidelines, clinical knowledge, and documentation improvement principles in real-world inpatient scenarios.

This guide breaks down everything you need to know to pass the CCDS exam on your first attempt — from eligibility requirements and exam structure to a domain-by-domain study strategy.

CCDS Exam Format at a Glance

The CCDS exam consists of 140 multiple-choice questions with four answer options (A through D). Of those, 120 are scored and 20 are unscored pretest questions embedded throughout the exam — you won't know which are which.

Questions fall into three cognitive levels:

  • Recall (~40%) — Testing your knowledge of specific facts, guidelines, and definitions relevant to CDI work.
  • Application (~40%) — Requiring you to apply guidelines, coding rules, or clinical knowledge to a specific scenario.
  • Analysis (~20%) — Evaluating your ability to synthesize multiple pieces of information to solve a problem.

The exam is administered at Prometric testing centers or via remote proctoring. You are permitted to bring two reference books (checked at the door, no loose notes allowed).

Eligibility Requirements

ACDIS requires candidates to meet one of three education-and-experience pathways:

  1. RN, RHIA, RHIT, MD, or DO with at least 2 years of experience as a concurrent or retrospective documentation specialist in an inpatient acute care facility using the U.S. IPPS.
  2. Associate degree in an allied health field (other than above) with 3 years of inpatient CDI experience. Must include college-level coursework in medical terminology and anatomy/physiology.
  3. Formal education in medical terminology, anatomy/physiology, and pathophysiology — or an AHIMA CCS or CCS-P credential — with 3 years of inpatient CDI experience.
Exam Cost The CCDS exam fee is $280 for ACDIS members and $380 for non-members. If you fail, the first retake is discounted to $150 (with a 90-day waiting period). International candidates pay an additional $100.

The Seven Core Competency Areas

ACDIS identified seven core competencies through industry surveys and expert advisory boards. The exam handbook further organizes content into eight testable sections. Here is what each major area covers and how to study it.

1. Healthcare Regulations, Reimbursement, and IPPS

This domain tests your understanding of how documentation connects to reimbursement under the Inpatient Prospective Payment System. You need to know how MS-DRGs are assigned, the role of CCs and MCCs, and the financial impact of documentation specificity. Study CMS guidelines, DRG logic, and how clinical documentation affects case-mix index.

2. Clinical Indicators and Disease Processes

Expect questions about clinical signs, symptoms, and diagnostic criteria for conditions commonly targeted in CDI — sepsis, heart failure, respiratory failure, malnutrition, and acute kidney injury among others. A solid foundation in pathophysiology is essential. Review clinical criteria definitions (especially sepsis per Sepsis-3) and understand the lab values and vital signs that support diagnoses.

3. Medical Record Documentation

This area covers documentation requirements, what constitutes adequate physician documentation, and the principles of accurate and complete medical records. Know the difference between clinical documentation and coding documentation, and understand what elements make a record query-worthy.

4. CDI Program Management and Analysis

Questions in this domain address how CDI programs are structured, measured, and improved. Be familiar with CDI metrics like query response rates, agreement rates, case-mix impact, and how to analyze program data to identify documentation improvement opportunities.

5. Communication and Physician Education

This is where query writing lives. You need to understand compliant query practices per the ACDIS/AHIMA guidelines, how to engage physicians effectively, and the principles of education-based CDI outreach. Know the difference between compliant and leading queries.

6. Coding Guidelines and Official Coding Rules

You must be familiar with ICD-10-CM Official Guidelines for Coding and Reporting and relevant AHA Coding Clinic guidance. Focus on guidelines related to conditions CDI specialists frequently query — present on admission indicators, principal diagnosis selection, and sequencing rules for complications and comorbidities.

7. Professionalism, Ethics, and Quality of Care

The final areas address ethical CDI practice, compliance with regulations, and the expanding role of CDI in quality-of-care measures. Understand how documentation impacts quality metrics, value-based purchasing, and mortality indices like PSI-90 and Elixhauser scores.

Recommended Study Timeline

Most successful candidates study for 6 to 10 weeks with a structured plan. Here's a suggested breakdown:

  • Weeks 1–2: Review regulations, IPPS, and DRG methodology. Take an initial practice assessment to identify weak spots.
  • Weeks 3–4: Deep dive into clinical indicators and disease processes. Use clinical case scenarios for practice.
  • Weeks 5–6: Focus on coding guidelines and query compliance. Study ICD-10-CM guidelines and Coding Clinic entries.
  • Weeks 7–8: Cover CDI program analysis, physician communication, and ethics/quality domains.
  • Weeks 9–10: Full-length timed practice exams. Review explanations for every wrong answer. Focus remaining study time on your weakest domains.
Study Tip Don't just memorize facts — the exam emphasizes application and analysis. Practice with scenario-based questions that force you to think through clinical situations, not just recall definitions.

Top 5 Mistakes CCDS Candidates Make

  1. Over-relying on passive reading. Study guides are helpful for content review, but you need active practice with exam-style questions to build test-taking skills.
  2. Ignoring the coding domains. Candidates from clinical backgrounds sometimes underestimate the coding portion. ICD-10-CM guidelines and Coding Clinic questions appear heavily on the exam.
  3. Not practicing under timed conditions. The exam is 3.5 hours for 140 questions. If you never practice with a timer, pacing on exam day can be a shock.
  4. Skipping the quality-of-care section. This is a newer exam domain and candidates often deprioritize it. Don't — it appears on the exam.
  5. Studying without a plan. Spreading study time randomly across topics is far less effective than a structured, domain-by-domain approach with practice tests at intervals.

What to Bring on Exam Day

You are allowed to bring two reference books into the testing center. Most candidates bring the ICD-10-CM Official Guidelines for Coding and Reporting and the AHA Coding Clinic. Books are inspected for loose papers or additional inserts, which are not allowed. Handwritten notes in the margins and tabs are permitted.

Arrive early, bring valid government-issued ID, and know the Prometric testing center procedures if testing in person.

Frequently Asked Questions

How hard is the CCDS exam?

The CCDS exam is considered moderately difficult. The mix of recall, application, and analysis questions means you can't pass on memorization alone. Candidates with 2+ years of CDI experience and a structured study plan typically do well.

How long do results take?

Results are typically available within 4 to 6 weeks after your exam date. You'll receive an email notification from ACDIS.

Can I retake the exam if I fail?

Yes. You must wait 90 days before retaking the exam. The first retake fee is discounted to $150.

Is the CCDS exam open book?

Partially. You can bring two approved reference books (typically coding guidelines and Coding Clinic), but the exam is not "open book" in the traditional sense — you won't have time to look up every answer.
