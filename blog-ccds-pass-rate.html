CCDS Exam Pass Rate: What You Need to Know

Updated April 2026 • 8 min read

One of the most common questions from CDI specialists preparing for the CCDS exam is: what's the pass rate? The answer requires some nuance, because ACDIS doesn't publicly publish official pass rate statistics the way some other certification bodies do.

Here's what we know — and what it means for your preparation.

What the Available Data Tells Us

ACDIS does not routinely publish the CCDS exam pass rate. However, based on historical ACDIS conference presentations, industry discussions, and survey data from CDI professionals, the estimated first-attempt pass rate is generally believed to fall in the 65% to 75% range.

That means roughly one in four first-time test-takers does not pass. This is consistent with a moderately difficult professional certification exam — comparable to exams like the CCS (Certified Coding Specialist) and tougher than basic HIM credentialing exams.

Key Takeaway The CCDS exam is not something you can wing with CDI experience alone. Candidates who use structured study materials and practice questions consistently outperform those who rely solely on on-the-job knowledge.

Why Candidates Fail

After talking with hundreds of CDI professionals about their exam experience, the most common reasons for failing the CCDS exam are:

1. Underestimating the Coding Content

Candidates from nursing or clinical backgrounds often feel confident about the clinical portions but struggle with ICD-10-CM guidelines, Coding Clinic entries, and DRG methodology. The coding sections are heavily represented on the exam, and clinical knowledge alone isn't enough to answer them correctly.

2. Not Practicing Under Timed Conditions

The exam gives you 3.5 hours for 140 questions — roughly 90 seconds per question. That sounds generous until you're working through a complex clinical scenario with multiple data points to consider. Candidates who never practice with a timer frequently run into pacing problems.

3. Over-Reliance on Reference Books

Yes, you can bring two reference books into the exam. But candidates who plan to "look everything up" during the test almost always run out of time. Your reference books should be a safety net for specific coding guidelines — not your primary source of answers. The knowledge needs to be in your head first.

4. Ignoring the Newer Exam Domains

The quality-of-care section and CDI program analysis domains are newer additions to the exam. Candidates who studied with older materials or focused only on clinical and coding content can be caught off guard by questions about quality metrics, PSI indicators, and CDI program benchmarking.

5. Studying Without Practice Questions

This is perhaps the single biggest differentiator between candidates who pass and those who don't. Passive reading (even thorough reading) does not prepare you for the way exam questions are structured. The CCDS exam uses scenario-based questions that require you to analyze a situation and select the best answer — and that's a skill you build through repetition, not reading.

What First-Time Passers Do Differently

Based on patterns from successful candidates, the common threads are:

  • They use a structured study plan with defined weeks for each domain, rather than studying randomly.
  • They practice with scenario-based questions regularly — not just at the end, but throughout their study period.
  • They review explanations for every question — including the ones they got right — to reinforce reasoning, not just memorization.
  • They take full-length timed practice exams at least twice before test day to calibrate their pacing.
  • They focus extra time on their weakest domains rather than continuing to study what they already know well.
  • They tab and organize their reference books in advance so they can find specific guidelines quickly during the exam without wasting time.

How to Predict If You're Ready

The most reliable readiness indicator is your performance on full-length practice exams under timed conditions. Here's a general benchmark:

  • Scoring 80%+ consistently on practice exams = strong chance of passing.
  • Scoring 70–79% = you're in the zone but should continue drilling weak areas.
  • Scoring below 70% = you likely need more study time before testing.

These benchmarks assume your practice questions are calibrated to exam difficulty. Questions that are too easy will give you false confidence; questions that are unrealistically hard will cause unnecessary anxiety.

What Happens If You Don't Pass

If you fail the CCDS exam, you can retake it after a 90-day waiting period. The first retake is discounted to $150 (versus the standard $280/$380 fee). You'll receive a score report that indicates your performance by content area, which helps you focus your additional study time.

Failing isn't the end of the world — but it does cost you time and money. That's why investing in proper preparation upfront is almost always more efficient than retaking.

The Bottom Line on CCDS Pass Rates

The CCDS exam is passable — but it requires deliberate preparation. The estimated 65–75% first-attempt pass rate means that roughly a quarter of candidates don't pass on their first try, and the evidence strongly suggests that the difference comes down to preparation quality, not raw intelligence or experience level.

Structured study, consistent question practice, and timed exam simulation are the three pillars that give candidates the best shot at passing on the first attempt.
